What Were the Meditations Really For?

Marcus Aurelius's Meditations stand as a profound philosophical tapestry, intricately woven with reflections on virtuous living, the universe, and navigating life's intricacies. At its core, the Meditations encapsulate Stoic principles, aiming to guide individuals toward moral integrity, self-awareness, and acceptance of the cosmic order. Aurelius contemplates the interconnectedness of existence, asserting that everything is interwoven, and the web is holy, with none of its parts unconnected. This foundational idea underscores the unity and coherence within the world, a central concept in Stoic thought.

Hadot delves into the nature and purpose of these meditations, presenting them not merely as a personal diary but as a philosophical exercise — an instructional guide for living in harmony with nature, central to Stoicism. He emphasizes how Aurelius utilizes the meditations as tools for self-examination, self-mastery, and self-transformation, indicating their intent as a roadmap toward self-awareness and alignment with one's inherent nature. Annas offers a complementary perspective, viewing the meditations as a record of Aurelius's struggles in applying Stoic principles to his life and circumstances. She highlights how the meditations serve as a testament to Aurelius's endeavor to navigate the complexities of existence while adhering to Stoic ideals of rationality and virtue.

The spiritual exercises within the meditations, according to Hadot, encompass techniques aimed at cultivating a cosmic perspective and nurturing a rational and virtuous disposition. These exercises facilitate the alignment of one's consciousness with the order of the cosmos. Annas, on the other hand, emphasizes the practicality of these exercises, focusing on the development of reasoning, judgment, and action to foster a coherent and consistent character conducive to a harmonious life.

Living according to nature, as expounded by Hadot, implies living in conformity with one's true nature — rational and social — and in harmony with the ordered and providential nature of the whole. Annas emphasizes the dynamic and diverse nature of both the individual and the world, advocating for living in accordance with one's inherent complexity and the ever-changing nature of the world.

Both Hadot and Annas highlight the challenges Stoicism faces. Hadot notes the criticisms from other philosophical schools, questioning Stoic assumptions in metaphysics, ethics, and logic. Annas addresses modern criticisms regarding Stoicism's applicability, relevance, and validity in contemporary contexts.

In essence, Aurelius's Meditations serve as a treasure trove of Stoic wisdom, embodying a journey toward ethical living, inner harmony, and a deeper understanding of one's place within the cosmic framework. Hadot and Annas offer complementary perspectives that enrich our comprehension of Aurelius's philosophical pursuits, shedding light on the practical applications and challenges inherent in Stoicism's timeless teachings. The Meditations are neither purely about virtuous life nor purely about understanding the universe; they are about both simultaneously, and it is precisely the insistence on holding both together that makes them distinctively Stoic.
