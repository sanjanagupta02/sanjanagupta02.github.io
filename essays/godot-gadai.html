When Godot Becomes Gadai: Vernacularisation, Philosophical Loss, and the Indian Stage

Samuel Beckett spent a significant portion of his later life fighting to maintain control over productions of Waiting for Godot. He objected to directorial interventions that gave the play a recognisable landscape, resisted casting choices that made the characters legible social types, and, on at least one occasion, threatened legal action against a production he felt had violated the text (Knowlson 1996). The resistance was philosophical. The play works because it refuses to locate itself: there is no country, no specific poverty, no named god, no identifiable Godot. When Theatre Workshop's Ashok Mukhopadhyay brought the play to stages across Kolkata, in 2026 as Gadaibabu Ashchhen, with Goutam Halder and Loknath Dey in the lead roles, the production was, by any theatrical measure, a success. Having attended the performance, one came away struck by the quality of the dialogue, the physical intelligence of the two lead actors, and the way colloquial Bengali and intermittent English together created something that felt genuinely true to contemporary Kolkata life. The argument of this essay is not that the adaptation fails. It is that it transforms the ontology of waiting itself, substituting a recognisable social waiting for Beckett's metaphysical indeterminacy, and that this substitution, however productive on its own terms, changes what the play is doing at its deepest level. The question, more precisely, is this: what does it cost philosophically when Godot becomes Gadai?

The Grammar of Waiting in India

Prior to analyzing the changes made to the play by the adaptation, we must first comprehend why the play is so popular in India. Beckett drew inspiration for Waiting for Godot from the devastation of post-war Europe, yet Europe has no special claim to the state he described. Dipesh Chakrabarty's argument in Provincializing Europe is useful here in a precise way: he contends that European historicism places non-Western societies in a "not yet" position relative to modernity, consigning them to a kind of historical waiting room in which they are perpetually on the threshold of arriving at a destination that keeps receding (Chakrabarty 2000). This is not simply a metaphor. It describes the structural logic by which colonial and postcolonial states defer the fulfilment of their promises indefinitely, always pointing toward a future that justifies the deprivation of the present. The poor wait for the monsoon, for wages, for land titles. The dispossessed wait for courts, for reservations to translate into real access, for the promised scheme to actually begin. Partha Chatterjee, in The Politics of the Governed, documents how large sections of India's population navigate the state not through the language of rights and citizenship but through a politics of petition and supplication, an endless waiting at the margins of governmental attention for someone to notice (Chatterjee 2004). This is not the same as Beckett's existential waiting, and it would be a mistake to collapse the distinction, but the structural familiarity with deferral is why the play does not require the same contextualisation for many Indian audiences that it requires for audiences encountering it through the lens of post-war European crisis. The condition is already known, even if its philosophical framing is different.

One might object that this reading simply identifies a thematic similarity and calls it resonance, without accounting for what is specific to the play's formal operations. Martin Esslin's argument about the Theatre of the Absurd is relevant here: the plays in this tradition, he suggests, derive their force not from what they say about the world but from enacting the absence of meaning rather than describing it (Esslin 1961). The point is not that many Indian audiences recognise the theme of waiting but that the play's formal structure, its repetition, its refusal of resolution, its comic deflation of every attempt at significance, produces a particular experience that lands differently in different contexts. For many urban middle-class European audiences in 1953, this formal enactment of vacancy was a philosophical shock. For audiences in cities like Kolkata, for whom structural deferral is already a lived condition rather than an existential discovery, it might register as something closer to confirmation.

Pozzo, Pujo, and Sacred Authority

The renaming of characters across the adaptation is mostly straightforward. Bhola and Ashu are the two tramps, their names warm and familiar in ways Vladimir and Estragon are not, and the play does not ask these names to carry philosophical weight. The renaming of Pozzo as Pujo is different, though it is worth pausing first on the simpler explanation: Pozzo and Pujo sound similar enough that the name may have been chosen primarily for phonetic ease. If that is the whole story, what follows is moot. But the production's overall intelligence, and Mukhopadhyay's clearly stated awareness of the play's social registers, makes it difficult to believe the semantic weight of pujo was accidental.

In Bengali, pujo means worship, the devotional ritual of supplicating a deity. Pozzo in Beckett is the master: the whip-holder, the man with a rope around Lucky's neck, whose power degrades visibly across the two acts without ever releasing Lucky from his chain. The name Pozzo carries no cultural resonance in English or French; it signals authority and foreignness without attaching itself to anything specific. Naming him Pujo places the oppressor within the same semantic field as worship, which in the Indian context is not a metaphor but an actual social dynamic. Ashis Nandy, in The Intimate Enemy, traces how colonial and postcolonial authority operates not only through force but through its internalisation by the governed, how the master's power is gradually experienced not as imposition but as something closer to legitimate order (Nandy 1983). Chatterjee extends this into the contemporary bureaucratic state: the poor do not approach government functionaries as citizens asserting rights, he argues, but as petitioners seeking grace, in a mode structurally closer to religious supplication than to civic demand (Chatterjee 2004). This is the dynamic Pujo makes theatrical. The name fuses power and sanctified ritual into a single figure, and the effect is something Beckett's original does not foreground in the same way. One might counter that this reading attributes too much intentionality to a name that the average Bengali audience member may not consciously process as semantically significant. But theatrical meaning does not require conscious processing to operate; it works through association, and the association between pujo and the ritualism of approaching power is too embedded in Bengali cultural life to be neutralised by inattention.

Lucky becomes Luka, a slight softening that also brings the name into the Bengali register. In Beckett, Lucky is one of modern drama's most disturbing figures: a man so completely broken by servitude that his only extended speech is a torrent of degraded, fragmentary thought. Within the Pujo-Luka relationship, this takes on a specific social dimension the adaptation handles with more directness than Beckett's more existentially framed original. The peon, the daily-wage labourer, the landless agricultural worker who has lost the language in which to articulate grievance, these are not abstract figures in India, and Luka's inability to produce coherent speech except on Pujo's command maps onto the specific condition of those so thoroughly shaped by structures of exploitation that resistance becomes literally unspeakable. In naming the master Pujo and the servant Luka, the adaptation offers a more complete social anatomy: not only the sanctified authority of the oppressor but the class embodiment of the one whose voice has been taken away.

When Godot Becomes Gadai

Gadaibabu Ashchhen is not the first Indian production to wrestle with the problem of rendering Godot in a South Asian language and context. Habib Tanvir's engagement with the play in Hindi theatre, and several other regional productions over the past decades, have all faced the question of whether to transliterate "Godot" phonetically or to find a name that carries equivalent cultural weight. What makes Mukhopadhyay's choice distinctive is the specific social identity the name "Gadai" carries in Bengali.

Ruby Cohn argued that "Godot" is deliberately constructed to remain at the edge of recognition: close enough to seem meaningful, far enough to remain permanently unresolved (Cohn 1980). Throughout his life, Beckett declined to define Godot; this reluctance was a structural commitment rather than an authorial ploy. The symbol functions because it is empty, able to take in whatever the audience contributes without confirming or refuting anything (Esslin 1961). Godot must not be anyone in particular, because the play's formal operations, the repetition, the circularity, the non-arrival, depend on the audience being unable to fill the vacancy with a recognisable face.

Gadai is a Bengali man's name. It is the kind of name one might give a slightly ridiculous older neighbour, a local strongman, a figure from Bengali popular fiction. "Gadaibabu," with the honorific "babu" attached, places him in a specific Bengali social hierarchy: someone of middling standing who expects deference, who might plausibly be described as the sort of person who keeps you waiting without apology. One can picture Gadaibabu. One can speculate about where he is, why he has not come, whether he will show up tomorrow. The waiting is no longer suspended in a void; it is suspended in the specific, recognisable frustrations of Bengali social life. This is real and meaningful, but it is not the same thing as existential vacancy, and the difference matters for how the play's formal repetitions operate. In Beckett, the second act's repetition of the first produces a kind of vertigo, because there is nothing to hold onto: no Godot whose absence we can rationalise. In Gadaibabu Ashchhen, the repetition is more likely to produce something closer to bitter recognition, because Gadai's non-arrival is entirely plausible within the logic of the Bengali social world the production has constructed. Rustom Bharucha argues that cross-cultural theatrical borrowing always carries this consequence: the domesticating adaptation works well on its own terms while shedding the foreignness that gave the original its critical force, not as a failure but as a structural effect of making the unfamiliar familiar (Bharucha 1993). The foreignness of Godot, his radical unlocatability, is not one interpretive layer among others that can be exchanged for local resonance; it is the condition under which every other element of the play operates.

This brings us to a counter-argument the essay must honestly engage. Mukhopadhyay stated explicitly that he had no interest in making a cerebral exercise of the play. He wanted a ranga-natya, a festive, comedic spectacle in the tradition of Chaplin and Raj Kapoor, where suffering is performed as lightness (Majumder 2026). If the director was never trying to preserve Beckett's metaphysical void, then describing its absence as a "loss" may be applying the wrong evaluative framework. The response to this is not that the director's stated intentions settle the question. The philosophical consequences of naming choices are not fully within any director's control. When Godot becomes Gadai, the play's structure of unknowability shifts regardless of what the production wants to do with its other elements. What a director intends and what an adaptation produces are not always the same thing.

Highlights of the Play

The production's strengths were real and substantial. The dialogue was where Gadaibabu Ashchhen was most impressive. The colloquial Bengali carried the rhythms of working-class suburbs, the particular cadence of people who have learned to fill time with words because the alternative is sitting with how little they have. Bhola and Ashu's exchanges had the quality of two people who have run out of new things to say to each other but have not run out of the need to say something, which is precisely what Beckett's text requires. The physical comedy was grounded in the small, specific indignities of poverty, a borrowed shoe that does not fit, a hat passed back and forth as a gesture of dignity neither man can quite sustain, rather than the more abstract clowning of the source text, and it earned genuine laughter from the audience without releasing the undertow of melancholy the play also requires.

What stood out most was the intermittent use of English. Bureaucratic phrases and the register of official correspondence surfaced in the dialogue at unexpected moments. Saikat Majumdar, writing on the relationship between postcolonial culture and the languages of colonial modernity, argues that the banal registers of official and institutional life carry their own form of power, encoding hierarchies within the texture of everyday speech in ways that are difficult to resist precisely because they present themselves as neutral (Majumdar 2013). The code-switching in Gadaibabu Ashchhen operates within exactly this dynamic. For many urban Bengali working-class and lower-middle-class audiences, English is not only the language of aspiration or education; it is the language in which the state addresses you when it is about to deny you something. The form is in English. The rejection is in English. The fact that Bhola and Ashu reached for this English even in their helplessness, even when it offered them nothing, gave their waiting a precise social address that a purely Bengali dialogue could not have achieved in the same way.

Goutam Halder and Loknath Dey carried the production to success. In an interview, Dey stated that the play's main challenge is really one of "non-acting": maintaining an audience's interest through repetition and stillness as opposed to action (Majumder 2026). The dynamic of two people who have been waiting together long enough that their boredom has turned into a type of intimacy was really lived in by the physical shorthand between the two performers.

The Silence at the End

Bhabha's concept of the third space of enunciation is useful here not as a general resolution but as an analytical tool: translation between cultures does not simply transfer meaning from one location to another but produces something new in the gap between them, something that belongs fully to neither the original nor the receiving context (Bhabha 1994). What Gadaibabu Ashchhen produces in that space can be named with some specificity. The Pujo-figure of sanctified bureaucratic authority is one such production, a figure that Beckett's Pozzo gestures toward but develops in a different register. The Luka figure, embodying the class condition of those who have lost the language of grievance, gives the Pujo-Luka dynamic a social texture that the original handles more abstractly. The code-switched English of institutional deferral gives the characters' helplessness a precise social address. These are not compensations for Beckett's void; they are distinct things the adaptation creates, with their own weight and their own critical force.

What the production achieves differently, and what Beckett's original does that the adaptation does not fully replicate, comes down to the distinction the essay has been building toward. The silence at the end of the performance, as Bhola and Ashu remained onstage waiting for a Gadai who did not come, was still a silence, and many in the audience sat with it in a way that suggested the play's deepest structure had survived the crossing. But it was a social silence: the silence of recognition, of people who know exactly what it is to wait for someone who is not going to come, rather than the silence of people who have glimpsed an absence without a social name. Both silences are real. The essay's argument is simply that they are not the same silence, and the distance between them is the cost of making Godot into Gadai.
